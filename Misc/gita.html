A Lesson from the Gita: Ego

Exploring the Spiritual Side of Me

Say Bye-Bye to Your Ego

The Bhagvad Gita is the religious book of the Hindus. This article needs certain background knowledge (apologies for the jargon). It covers an aspect of Krishna's advice to Arjun.

The Gita clearly underscores the importance of letting go of one's ego. We tend to think of ourselves before anything else. We give our thoughts, emotions and aims the highest priority in our lives. This leads to dominance, rivalry, rudeness, meanness, distinction, jealousy, loftiness etc. Both egoists and egotists harm themselves in the long run.

Ego is a person’s sense of self-identity, self–consciousness, self-respect and image in society. An egoist cares solely about his/her image and doesn’t pay any heed to others’ point of view or their feelings.

In the Gita, Arjun is not compared to an egoist at all. On the contrary, Krishna explains to him that if one is facing any sort of a problem, one should let go of any ego or attachment and look at the situation from a very neutral perspective as this would solve things without any judgmental bias.

The best suited example to this, according to me, is the ambiguity in Arjun’s mind about one’s kinsmen. The fact that he did not want to harm Bhisma and Drona brings forth both justice and injustice when viewed from different perspectives. Then how is one to decide what to do in such situations? Take the side of one’s own blood relations or Dharma?

How does ego fit into the picture? The key answer lies in Arjun’s attitude of partiality. Bhisma and Drona belonged to the opponent’s side although the former was like his father while the latter was his Guru. He gave into wrong ways and stereotypical opinions of the society and that was precisely his weakness. The society would have found it preposterous and absurd to kill one’s very own. The fact that Arjun thought of ‘HIS’ people, before thinking of the other lives that he would be taking while waging the war, is in itself an implication of ‘ego’ where he places something that he is attached to i.e. his people, before Dharma.

If we look at the scenario from a detached observer’s perspective- we realize that any sense of attachment leads to bias decisions and prejudice. Is is Arjun’s fault? No, this is a human tendency. A detached person would never have viewed the ‘Kauravas’ as his/her brother and would therefore make a clear cut decision only based on dharma and adharma without any worldly intervention. Rightfully, Arjun should have been ready to kill Drona and Bhishma without hesitation, which he failed to do.

Bhishma or Drona may not have been at fault individually, but they joined the opposing side which made them rivals. There can never be any alterations, compromises or exceptions to Dharma for Dharma is the ultimate truth.

Being a Kshatriya warrior himself , Arjun never had any issues with fighting. He had an issue with the people who had challenged him. Isn’t violating both, non-violence and ahimsa, a kind of adharma in itself?

According to me, at that time caste was a mere excuse for dominancy. It was probably made to maintain guilds, family lines of expertise and a harmony or balance between people of various occupational fields and colours of life. He was a warrior by birth, but did that give him the right to kill? That question has not been raised at all. Instead, he is dropped into a no-win situation and is checkmated. He has to fight-for if he flees,it will result in the annihilation of the small Pandava clan. The best thing to do in such a situation is to forget any personal ties and treat all enemies alike.
